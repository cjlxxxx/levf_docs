The Problem

The best single-intervention lifespan results in mice started at middle age — rapamycin and calorie restriction — each extend median lifespan by roughly 3–4 months. This ceiling has held for over a decade. To dramatically exceed it, we need interventions that address damage these approaches do not.

Redundant interventions — those that work through the same biological channels — are unlikely to add materially to a combination. The challenge is that we lack complete mechanistic understanding of how any intervention extends lifespan. We cannot predict from first principles which interventions will be redundant and which will be additive.

The Approach

RMR2 is a phased longevity study that tests whether a carefully chosen combination of 8 interventions — each targeting a different type of age-related damage — can significantly exceed the rapamycin benchmark. Rather than assume biology, we use empirical inference from outcomes to guide decisions.

Key Comparisons

Every arm is compared against rapamycin as the benchmark. Three comparisons carry the core logic:

rX vs r
Does intervention X add anything to rapamycin?

Tests whether each intervention addresses damage rapamycin misses.

E vs r
Does the combination beat rapamycin?

The primary goal of the entire study.

rE vs E
Does rapamycin add to the combination?

Translation signal — if E ≈ rE, the benefit isn't rapamycin-dependent, increasing clinical flexibility.

The Nuance: When rX ≈ r

If adding X to rapamycin doesn't extend lifespan further, the naive interpretation is that X is redundant with rapamycin. But there are three competing explanations, and only one implies true redundancy:

1. Target Overlap

X works through the same pathway as rapamycin.

Truly redundant
2. Damage-Type Masking

X works independently, but the damage it fixes isn't rate-limiting after rapamycin.

Not redundant — may help without r
3. Network Masking

X's effects propagate through nodes rapamycin already addressed.

Not redundant — masked only in r context

Why this matters: Masking and network masking mean X may still work in humans, where the damage network topology differs. The framework uses secondary endpoints (biomarkers, mTOR markers, cause of death) to distinguish these explanations rather than defaulting to the "redundant" interpretation.

Best-Case Outcome

E >> r
AND
E ≈ rE

The combination dramatically exceeds the rapamycin benchmark, and rapamycin adds nothing — meaning the benefit is not dependent on mTOR inhibition. This would represent the first robust demonstration that combinatorial approaches to aging damage can break through the single-intervention ceiling, while maximizing flexibility for clinical development pathways.

Why This Design

Rapamycin as benchmark, not assumption. Using the best known single intervention as the comparator means every result is calibrated against the current state-of-the-art. We don't need to assume how rapamycin works — we use it as a yardstick.

Full combination first. Testing all 8 together before testing subsets avoids the combinatorial explosion of pairwise testing (28 pairs, 56 triples, …). If the combination works, Phase 2 efficiently identifies the essential components through subtraction.

Epistemically honest. The framework doesn't pretend to know which damage pathways are rate-limiting or how interventions interact at the network level. It uses outcomes to generate hypotheses, secondary endpoints to test them, and a structured decision tree to guide Phase 2.

Translation-aware. The rE vs E comparison directly tests whether the combination's benefit is rapamycin-dependent. If not, clinical development has more flexibility — important because rapamycin's immunosuppressive side effects complicate human use.
